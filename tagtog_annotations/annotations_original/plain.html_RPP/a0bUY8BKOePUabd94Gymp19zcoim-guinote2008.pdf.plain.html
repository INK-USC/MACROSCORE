ATTITUDES AND SOCIAL COGNITION Power and Affordances: When the Situation Has More Power Over Powerful Than Powerless Individuals Ana Guinote University of Kent Six studies examined how power affects responses to situational affordances. Participants were assigned to a powerful or a powerless condition and were exposed to various situations that afford different classes of behavior. Study 1 examined behavior intentions for weekdays and weekends. Studies 2 and 3 focused on responses to imaginary social and work situations. Study 4 examined planned behavior for winter and summer days. Finally, Studies 5 and 6 examined behavior and attention in the presence of situation- relevant and irrelevant information. Consistently across these studies, powerful individuals acted more in situation-consistent ways, and less in situation-inconsistent ways, compared with powerless individuals. These findings are interpreted as a result of the greater tendency for powerful individuals to process information selectively in line with the primary factors that drive cognition, such as affordances. One consequence of these findings is that powerful individuals change behavior across situations more than powerless individuals. Keywords: power, affordances, automatic goal activation, objective group variability, situated cognition Social scientists, policy analysts, and laypeople have long been interested in the ways in which power affects behavior. Power leads to action (Galinsky, Gruenfeld, & Magee, 2003), behavior disinhibition (Keltner, Gruenfeld, & Anderson, 2003), behavior variability (Guinote, Judd, & Brauer, 2002), and the facilitation of goal pursuit (Guinote, 2007c; see also Bargh & Raymond, 1995; Chen, Lee-Chai, & Bargh, 2001). In terms of the direction of behavior (i.e., what individuals actually do), research has, how- ever, often produced contradictory findings. Some studies indicate that power magnifies the expression of personal dispositions, pref- erences, and desires of the person (e.g., Chen et al., 2001). Other studies indicate that the behavior of powerful individuals is not necessarily driven by stable internal factors (e.g., Guinote, 2007d; Weick & Guinote, in press). Powerful individuals can act indiscriminately in any direction (Galinsky et al., 2003), change their judgments more as a function of subjective experiences (Weick & Guinote, in press), and act in more variable ways than powerless individuals (Guinote et al., 2002). For example, Galinsky et al. (2003, Experiment 3) found that when a task calls for one to give her or his own resources to a common good (e.g., in a public dilemma), powerful individuals give more resources compared with powerless individuals, thereby showing more prosocial behavior. However, when the task calls for taking resources out of a common good (e.g., in a commons dilemma), powerful individuals take more out of the common good compared with powerless individuals, thereby showing less proso- cial behavior. What remains unclear are the factors that account for the seemingly contradictory behavior of powerful individuals. One possible explanation for these findings is that powerful individuals respond more in line with the affordances of the situation1 com- pared with powerless individuals. Different situations are associated with the pursuit of different goals. For example, work environments afford work activities, whereas parties afford entertainment. Entering one’s office or a party triggers action tendencies that are consistent with these affordances (see J. J. Gibson, 1977; Smith & Semin, 2004; Tucker & Ellis, 1998). I follow a situated perspective of cognition (see Barsalou, 1999; Smith & Semin, 2004; see also Niedenthal, Barsalou, Winkielman, Karuth-Gruber, & Ric, 2005), arguing that cognition and behavior are dynamic and construed on a moment-to-moment basis. Cog- nition does not exist for its own sake and does not operate through static representations that describe external realities such as sche- mas, prototypes, stereotypes, and the like. The situated perspective of cognition highlights the role of environmental cues (e.g., affor- dances) as well as embodiment in guiding judgment and behavior. Before examining closer the links between power and responsive- ness to affordances of the situation, I first focus on the ways power affects cognition and how this, in turn, can promote responsiveness to the affordances provided by the situation. What Is Power? Power is a relational concept that can operate at the individual level (e.g., trait dominance), interpersonal level (e.g., social roles implying authority), and intergroup level (e.g., ethnicity, member- ship in a numerical majority or minority; Fiske & Berdahl, 2007; Keltner et al., 2003). A common feature of power across these levels of analysis is that powerful individuals enjoy a dispropor- tionate amount of resources (Kipnis, Castell, Gergen, & Mauch, 1976), control over others’ outcomes (Fiske, 1993; Fiske & Dépret, 1996; Thibaut & Kelley, 1959), and ability to influence others (Cartwright, 1959; French & Raven, 1959; Vescio, Snyder, & Butz, 2003). Power, Situated Cognition, and Behavior Individuals are exposed to a great amount of information in a given moment. However, due to limited capacity, only part of this information is processed depending on the primary factors that drive cognition in that moment (e.g., expectancies, goals, needs, experiential information, repeatedly used constructs, priming, af- fordances; see Driver, 2001; Posner & Snyder, 1975). Selective information processing is situated and occurs through patterns of activation of relevant information and inhibition of irrelevant information. In line with the Situated Focus Theory of Power (Guinote, 2007a; see also Guinote, 2007b), I argue that power increases the ability to process information selectively, and con- sequently the ability to behave in focused, prioritized ways in line with the factors that drive cognition in the situation (e.g., affor- dances, expectancies). Power changes the relationship individuals have with their en- vironment. The greater control over resources and over others that powerful individuals possess, the more they meet their own needs (Van Dijke & Poppe, 2006). These individuals are not dependent on external circumstances and are free from constraints (Fiske & Dépret, 1996; Keltner et al., 2003). They can focus attention on the task at hand and do not need to process all information available in the environment. According to the Situated Focus Theory of Power, powerful individuals have more cognitive resources to devote to the task at hand and are therefore better able to inhibit task-irrelevant infor- mation. Furthermore, powerful individuals trust default cognitive mechanisms that usually guide judgment and behavior, such as the automatic activation of goals by the environment (see Bargh, 1990; Bargh, Bond, Lombardi, & Tota, 1986). In contrast, powerless individuals have multiple concerns. These individuals need to pay attention not only to the task at hand but also to those that control their outcomes. The Situated Focus Theory of Power proposes that powerless individuals pay attention to multiple sources of information and therefore have less cogni- tive resources for the task at hand. Furthermore, these individuals engage more in controlled cognition to increase predictability and control (see also Fiske & Dépret, 1996; Keltner et al., 2003). The present proposal claims that these tendencies override automatic responses such as perception–behavior links and automatic goal activation (see Bargh, 1990; Bargh et al., 1986). In addition, even if some level of goal activation is reached, powerless individuals are less able to exert the executive function of inhibiting goal- irrelevant information (see Guinote, 2007a, 2007b, 2007c). This in turn further decreases goal-consistent behavior. The hypothesis that powerful individuals are better able to exercise the executive function of focusing attention on a target and inhibiting irrelevant information received support in a series of studies involving size estimation and object orientation (Guinote, 2007b). These studies also demonstrate that powerful individuals use more flexible information-processing strategies in line with task demands (see also Overbeck & Park, 2006; Vescio et al., 2003). In contrast, powerless individuals are defocused, pay more attention to detail, are less flexible across tasks, and cannot ignore irrelevant information even when the task requires that they do so. Finally, the ability to inhibit irrelevant information facilitates ac- tion (see Guinote, 2007b, Experiment 2). In other words, focused attention leads to focused behavior. In contrast, attention to mul- tiple sources of information creates multiple sources of action control and less clear priorities (see Kuhl, 1986, 1992). Affordances can, therefore, be the sole source of action control for powerful individuals, whereas for powerless individuals, affor- dances compete with other sources of information for the control of action. As a consequence, I hypothesize that when affordances are a central feature of the situation, powerful individuals respond more in line with the affordances of the situation compared with powerless individuals. Affordances Guide Behavior The environment provides specific opportunities for action (i.e., affordances; J. J. Gibson, 1977). For instance, a summer day affords more outdoor activities than a winter day. When the person enters the situation, the situation automatically activates the activ- ities and goals that can be carried out in it (Aarts & Dijksterhuis, 2003; Craighero, Fadiga, Rizzolatti, & Umilta, 1998; Funder & Colvin, 1991; B. Gibson & Werner, 1994; J. J. Gibson, 1977; Mischel, 1968; Prinz, 1997). Although individuals can act in ways that are inconsistent with the situation, doing so is usually not functional. Cognition is situated and serves adaptive action (Smith & Semin, 2004; see also Fiske, 1992). The pursuit of the goals activated by the situation unfolds and runs to completion in similar ways to the pursuit of goals activated through an act of will (e.g., Aarts & Dijksterhuis, 2003; Bargh, 1990; Bargh, Gollwitzer, Lee-Chai, Barndollar, & Troetschel, 2001; Chartrand & Bargh, 1996). Goals activated by the situation produce the same outcomes, the same content-free features of goal pursuit, such as focused action and persistence, as the pursuit of similar goals set through an act of will (see Bargh, 1990; Bargh et al., 2001). It follows that the same effects of power observed in the pursuit of self-set goals (Guinote, 2007c) should occur when goals are activated by the situation. Specifically, power should lead to focused attention and prioritized behavior consistent with the focal goals activated by the situation. Power, Affordances, and Past Research I follow a situated cognition perspective (see Barsalou, 1999; Niedenthal et al., 2005; Smith & Semin, 2004), arguing that cognition evolves on a moment-to-moment basis and is responsive to environmental cues. This perspective does not, however, nec- essarily exclude some enduring person-driven influences on judg- ment and behavior (see Barsalou, 1999). Accordingly, power can magnify the expression of traits, dis- positions, and goals, as shown by past research (see Bargh & Raymond, 1995; Chen et al., 2001; Guinote, 2007c; Keltner et al., 2003). This occurs when person-driven influences guide cognition, such as when individuals pursue a goal driven by an act of will. However, when external influences are strong enough to guide cognition (e.g., when the situation is associated strongly with particular classes of behavior, and individuals enter the situation without preexisting goals that oppose the situation), power can magnify responses in line with the external influences. Consistent with these hypotheses, Guinote and Weick (in press) examined the effects of different primes on responses to social targets. After a neutral prime, powerful participants responded to the social target more in line with chronic dispositions than pow- erless participants. However, when the prime activated constructs that opposed the participants’ chronic dispositions, powerful par- ticipants were more guided by the prime (and less by the their chronic dispositions) than powerless participants. These effects derive from the more focused and selective information processing of powerful compared with powerless individuals. The present perspective argues that power affects primarily process-related aspects of information processing with implica- tions for the ways individuals behave. This perspective is not, however, inconsistent with content-based effects of power. Simi- larly, as in other domains (e.g., anxiety), attentional focus, or the ability to focus attention and inhibit irrelevant information, and attentional biases, or a heightened sensitivity to particular contents, are separate mechanisms (see Eysenck, Derakshan, Santos, & Calvo, 2007). For example, powerful individuals may have more cognitive capacity (as proposed by the Situated Focus Theory of Power; Guinote, 2007a) and also be more inclined to rely on stereotypes to maintain their status quo (as suggested by Fiske and her col- leagues; see Fiske & Dépret, 1996). Similarly, powerless individ- uals may have greater attentional demands due to the constraints they face (Guinote, 2007b) and at the same time be more sensitive to threats in the environment (Keltner et al., 2003). More impor- tant, the attentional focus that derives from power has conse- quences of its own for judgment and behavior. In the present article, I examine implications for responsiveness to affordances. The Present Study In the present study, I examined the ways powerful and pow- erless individuals respond to situational affordances. Specifically, I examined macro-affordances such as seasonal behavior, entertainment-, or work-directed behavior. I hypothesize that, compared with powerless individuals, powerful individuals will respond more in situation-consistent ways and will respond less in situation-inconsistent ways. For these individuals, the mere per- ception of the situation will automatically activate applicable con- structs and guide the individual’s selective attention toward the affordances of the situation. Powerful individuals should therefore invest their effort in the pursuit of the goals activated by the situation and should behave less in ways that are inconsistent with the situation. For example, considering that summer affords more outdoor activities and positive mood than winter, one would expect powerful individuals to engage more in outdoor activities and improve their mood in the summer compared with in the winter, whereas for powerless individuals, seasonal differences should be less pronounced. I tested these hypotheses in six studies. Participants were as- signed to a powerful or a powerless condition and were then exposed to a variety of situations that afford the pursuit of different goals. In four studies, participants were exposed to different situ- ations, and I subsequently examined the extent to which they responded in ways that were consistent and inconsistent with the situation. In a further study, participants were exposed to a situa- tion and to incidental information that was either consistent or inconsistent with the situation, and I examined how this informa- tion affected their behavior. Finally, in another study, participants were exposed to different situations, and I examined attention to cues that were relevant or irrelevant to the pursuit of the goals afforded by the situations. Imaginary situations were examined in three studies (Studies 2, 3, and 6), and intended behavior in future situations was examined in three studies (Studies 1, 4, and 5). Power was operationalized through control over outcomes (Studies 1 and 2), through group size (Study 3), and through priming of power or powerlessness (Studies 4, 5, and 6). If power promotes the pursuit of the goals activated by the situation, then powerful individuals should plan more leisure activities and less routine for their weekends and more routine and less leisure for their weekdays. General Discussion One of the primary goals of personality and social psychology research is to investigate how individuals modulate their behavior across the different situations they experience in their daily life (Andersen & Chen, 2002; J. J. Gibson, 1977; Mischel, 2000; Shoda & Mischel, 1993; Snyder, 1983). Research demonstrates that different situations activate different goals that can be carried out in the situation (J. J. Gibson, 1977; Prinz, 1997). For example, the workplace instigates work-related goals, a party instigates entertainment, and good weather instigates outdoor activities. Once goals have been activated by the situation, they then run to completion in similar ways as goals set by an act of will (Bargh, 1990; Bargh et al., 2001; Chartrand & Bargh, 1996; see also Aarts & Dijksterhuis, 2003; Prinz, 1997). In the present article, I hypothesized that power increases re- sponsiveness to the affordances of the situation. In line with findings indicating that affordances guide cognition and behavior (J. J.Gibson, 1977; Prinz, 1997; Tucker & Ellis, 1998) and that power facilitates reliance on the factors that drive cognition in the situation (Guinote, 2007a; see also Guinote, 2007b), I reasoned that powerful individuals would respond more in line with the affordances of the situation compared with powerless individuals. Accordingly, I expected powerful individuals, more than power- less individuals, to direct their attention and effort to the pursuit of the goals activated by the situation and respond less in ways that are irrelevant to the situation. To test these hypotheses, participants were assigned to a pow- erful or a powerless condition and exposed to situations that activate different goals. I then measured the extent to which participants behaved, and processed information, in ways that were consistent and inconsistent with the situation. Across six studies conducted in three different countries, using different manipula- tions of power and a variety of everyday life situations, I consis- tently found support for the present hypotheses. Studies 2, 3, and 6 revealed that individuals who occupied a powerful position responded more to imaginary social and work situations in line with what the situations afforded. Specifically, for powerful indi- viduals, more than for powerless individuals, the exposure to an internship situation activated primarily work activities (rather than social activities), and the exposure to the visit of a friend activated primarily social activities (rather than work activities). Behavioral intentions by participants assigned to a powerful or a powerless condition were examined in Studies 1, 4, and 5. The results showed that the intentions of powerful individuals were more focused on pursuing goals that were consistent with the situation. For example, powerful individuals were in a better mood and planned more outdoor and more vigorous activities in the summer than in the winter. In contrast, the activities and mood of powerless individuals were less affected by the situation. Finally, the results of Studies 5 and 6 clearly lend support for the hypothesis that a mechanism that contributes to the greater respon- siveness to situational affordances found in powerful individuals consists in the ability to focus attention on information that is relevant, and ignore information that is irrelevant, for the affor- dances of the situation. In Study 5, powerful individuals’ prefer- ence for leisurely activities during their summer vacation was much less affected by irrelevant alternatives than was the prefer- ence by powerless individuals. Power, Cognition, and Behavior One contribution of the present work is that it shows that power affects the choices of behavior individuals engage in. In addition to facilitating action (Galinsky et al., 2003; Guinote et al., 2002; Keltner et al., 2003), power also affects what individuals actually do. Powerful individuals prioritize and are better able at shielding their focal goals against potential intrusion (see Shah, Friedman, & Kruglanski, 2002). These results are consistent with findings ob- tained in the pursuit of goals activated through an act of will (Guinote, 2007c). The situation plays a more important role in initiating and organizing the behavior of powerful compared with powerless individuals. The individuals in powerful positions, compared with the individuals in powerless positions, are more likely to respond with what Jones and Gerard (1967) called an unequivocal behavior orientation toward the different situations they encounter in their everyday life. During work, they become fully engaged in work, whereas during the weekend, they become fully engaged in enter- tainment. The powerful individuals thus respond in an if–then fashion, discriminately conditioning their response on the nature of the situation (Mischel, 2000; Shoda & Mischel, 1993). As the present studies suggest, powerless individuals will sometimes en- tertain themselves during work rather than during the weekend, and work during the weekend rather than at the workplace. The present findings are consistent with the Situated Focus Theory of Power (Guinote, 2007a, 2007b), which claims that powerful individuals respond more selectively in line with the primary factors that drive cognition in the situation (e.g., expect- ancies, goals, frequently used constructs, needs, affordances). For example, when expectancies are activated, powerful individuals process more extensively information that is relevant to these expectancies and process less irrelevant information. Similarly, when goals are activated, either through an act of will or through exposure to affordances, powerful individuals respond more in a goal-consistent manner and respond less in a goal-inconsistent manner. In contrast, powerless individuals are not focused and are less guided by any single factor that drives cognition. According to this perspective, powerful individuals are more flexible and use a wider range of cognitive processes to guide their judgments and action. For example, they use high order, concep- tually driven cognition for the purpose of planning, problem solv- ing, and goal striving. However, they also trust lower order cog- nition in situations for which lower order cognition usually provides adaptive, good enough responses. For example, powerful individuals rely more on affective states (Hecht & LaFrance, 1998), experiential information (Guinote, 2007d; Weick & Gui- note, in press), and, as the present article shows, environmental cues. In contrast, powerless individuals tend to engage in con- trolled cognition (see also Fiske & Depret, 1996; Keltner et al., 2003), which overrides the influence of other processes. Power signals to the individual that desired outcomes will be attained and the environment is benign. Therefore, automatic re- sponses to the situation are adaptive for these individuals. In contrast, for powerless individuals, being guided by controlled cognition in order to increase predictability and control seems a more adaptive response. Another perspective that predicts action facilitation in powerful individuals is the approach-avoidance theory of power (Keltner et al., 2003; see also Anderson & Berdahl, 2002). This perspective proposes that power increases behavioral disinhibition, sensitivity to rewards, and positive affect, whereas powerlessness induces behavioral inhibition, sensitivity to threats, and negative affect. The Situated Focus Theory of Power is process related. It claims that the effects of power derive from the relative constraints that individuals experience in attaining valuable outcomes and the direct consequences for cognition. This approach builds on past research on control (e.g., Pittman & Pittman, 1980; Skinner, 1995; see also Fiske & Depret, 1996) and is based on the direct effects that power has on the individual’s goals system (i.e., their relative ability to attain desired outcomes). In contrast, the approach- avoidance theory claims that power affects the evaluation of stim- uli in the environment (i.e., affects the evaluative system), which, in turn, leads to approach or avoidance behavior. In spite of these differences, the two perspectives are compati- ble. Attentional focus, driven by differences in the objective dif- ficulties and demands that powerful and powerless individuals experience, and attentional bias, as a heightened sensitivity to certain contents (e.g., rewards, stereotypes), are two different mechanisms (see Eysenck et al., 2007). Powerful and powerless individuals may differ in processing capacity and distractibility and simultaneously be motivated to attain rewards or avoid threats. Furthermore, it is possible that powerless individuals’ avoidant orientation further impairs their processing abilities, in addition to the cognitive impairment that directly derives from objective con- straints. Situated focus proposes that powerful and powerless individuals pursue the same goals differently. Another perspective that also focuses on the ways individuals pursue goals, beyond the search for pleasure and avoidance of pain, is the Regulatory Focus Theory (Higgins, 1997). Accordingly, powerful and powerless may pursue goals differently. For example, a member of a dominant group may engage in academic goals to attain hopes and aspirations, follow- ing a promotion focus, whereas a member of a subordinate group may engage in academic goals as a result of concerns with the ought self, duties and safety, following a prevention focus. The relative contributions of situated focus, approach-avoidance, and regulatory focus for the behavior of powerful and powerless indi- viduals remain a topic for future research. The Power of the Person Versus the Power of the Situation Researchers have argued that power magnifies the expression of personal dispositions (Chen et al., 2001), the pursuit of personal goals (Bargh & Raymond, 1995), and resistance against external circumstances (Brauer & Bourhis, 2006; Lewin, 1941). According to this position, power increases control of the person over the situation. In contrast, the present findings point out that powerful individ- uals are more affected by the situation. Although the present and past research seem contradictory, this is not necessarily the case. Present research and past research have examined behavior from different perspectives. Prior research did not examine the role of situational affordances. For example, this research focused on chronically accessible constructs (Chen et al., 2001) or goals set by the person (Guinote, 2007c). Similarly, the present research did not examine how individuals respond to situations when they pursue self-initiated goals unrelated to the affordances of the situation. Under these conditions, powerful individuals may not respond to the affordances of the situation. These questions remain for future research. More important, the present research points out an ironic effect of power. In spite of the fact that powerful individuals often impose their will over others and resist external influences (e.g., Lewin, 1941), the situation may often have more power over powerful than powerless individuals.
